    About this tool

    Prepararsi al Presente is a research project exploring how dystopian attributes are represented in literary works across languages and AI annotation systems. Three large language models — Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini — annotated a corpus of dystopian texts in four languages (English, Spanish, Japanese, and a pooled Global view). This tool visualises the resulting attribute co-occurrence graphs: nodes are semantic attributes, edges connect attributes that appear together in the same work, weighted by frequency.

    How to navigate

    Landing page
    Overview of all 12 graphs (3 models × 4 languages). Click any card to open the single-graph view. Use the buttons at the top to compare models across a fixed language, compare languages across a fixed model, or open a custom two-graph comparison. The Graph similarity section at the bottom shows pairwise similarity matrices between all graphs.
    Single graph view
    Explore one graph in detail. The sidebar lets you filter by edge weight, change visual encodings, toggle labels, and click nodes to inspect their metrics and connections.
    Compare by dimension
    Shows 3 or 4 graphs side by side, fixing either the language (to compare models) or the model (to compare languages). Clicking a node highlights it across all graphs simultaneously, revealing which co-occurrence patterns are shared and which are model- or language-specific.
    Custom comparison ×2
    Pick any two graphs freely and compare them side by side. Useful for targeted cross-model or cross-language contrasts.

    Graph controls

    Min. edge weight
    Hides edges whose co-occurrence count is below the threshold. Raising it reveals the strongest structural backbone; lowering it shows rarer associations.
    Edge colour
    By weight: darker = more frequent co-occurrence. Source category: colour follows the semantic category of the source node. Degree partition: four quantile classes from low (light blue) to high (red) weighted degree.
    Node colour
    Category: each of the 20 semantic categories has a fixed colour. Community (Louvain): nodes are coloured by automatically detected cluster — see the legend for membership.
    Node size
    Uniform, or scaled by one of the computed measures: eigenvector centrality, clustering coefficient, hub score, or authority score.
    Labels
    Toggle the display of node names on all nodes at once. When labels are off, hovering a node shows its name and metrics in a temporary label.
    Interaction
    Click a node to pin the highlight and see details in the sidebar. Drag to reposition and pin a node; double-click to unpin it. Scroll / pinch to zoom; drag the background to pan.

    Graph measures

    Eigenvector centrality per node
    A node's importance is proportional to the sum of its neighbours' importance. High EC means the attribute not only co-occurs frequently but does so with other structurally central attributes. Computed via power iteration on the weighted adjacency matrix.
    Clustering coefficient per node
    The fraction of a node's neighbour pairs that are also directly connected. Values near 1 indicate the node sits inside a tight clique; values near 0 indicate a hub-and-spoke structure where neighbours do not connect to each other.
    Hub score & Authority score per node
    From the HITS algorithm. An authority is a node pointed to by many high-hub nodes; a hub points to many high-authority nodes. On an undirected weighted graph the two scores converge to similar values (close to eigenvector centrality), but may diverge if the graph is made directed in future work.
    Louvain community per node
    Automatically detected cluster using the Louvain greedy modularity optimisation. Nodes in the same community co-occur more densely with each other than with the rest of the graph. Communities are independent per graph — "Community 0" in Claude-en is not necessarily the same set of attributes as "Community 0" in ChatGPT-en.
    Modularity Q per graph
    Quality score of the community partition, shown in the header when viewing a single graph. Values above ~0.3 indicate meaningful community structure; near 0 means the graph is close to random; negative values are rare and indicate fewer internal edges than expected by chance.
    Weighted Jaccard similarity between graphs
    For each pair of graphs, computed as Σ min(w₁, w₂) / Σ max(w₁, w₂) over all edges. Measures overlap in both edge structure and weights. A value of 1 means the two graphs are identical; 0 means they share no edges.
    Cosine similarity of EC vectors between graphs
    Treats each graph's eigenvector centrality scores as a vector over the shared node set, then computes the cosine similarity between two such vectors. Measures whether two graphs agree on which attributes are most central, regardless of the absolute scale of the weights. High cosine similarity with low Jaccard means the graphs have different edges but similar structural hierarchies.
